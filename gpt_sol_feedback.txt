Short version: this is a promising numerical reformulation of the Krasikov–Lagarias method, but the “record already broken” language is premature. The supplied program really does reproduce their published numbers and produces the stated \(k=12,13,14\) values. It does not yet prove them.

### What problem is being counted?

Fix \(a\not\equiv0\pmod 3\), and let

\[
\pi_a(x)=\#\{n\le x:\text{the Collatz orbit of }n\text{ eventually reaches }a\}.
\]

Krasikov and Lagarias proved, using systems of difference inequalities and a computer-assisted feasibility calculation, that

\[
\pi_a(x)\ge x^{0.84}
\]

for all sufficiently large \(x\), with the threshold depending on \(a\). Their method actually found a numerical growth parameter around

\[
\lambda_{11}=1.792231,\qquad
\log_2\lambda_{11}=0.8417566,
\]

then stated the safely rounded exponent \(0.84\). Their Theorem 2.2 is the bridge from a feasible finite-dimensional system to the counting bound. [Krasikov–Lagarias paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/math/0205002)

### What the nonlinear Perron idea says

Ordinary Perron–Frobenius theory studies a nonnegative matrix \(A\) and its dominant eigenvalue:

\[
Ac=rc.
\]

Here the recurrence includes minima, so it is not a single matrix. It is a nonlinear map \(F\) assembled from nonnegative linear expressions and coordinatewise minima:

\[
F(c)=r\,c.
\]

Such a map is:

- monotone: \(c\le d\Rightarrow F(c)\le F(d)\);
- positively homogeneous: \(F(tc)=tF(c)\) for \(t>0\).

Nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory associates a spectral growth rate with maps of this type. Candidate vectors give ratio bounds of the form

\[
\min_i\frac{F(c)_i}{c_i}
\;\le r(F)\le\;
\max_i\frac{F(c)_i}{c_i}.
\]

Gaubert and Gunawardena prove eigenvector existence under additional connectivity/boundedness hypotheses—not literally for every monotone homogeneous map without qualification. Naive power iteration also does not universally converge merely from monotonicity and homogeneity. [Gaubert–Gunawardena](https://arxiv.org/abs/math/0105091)

The amusing “Collatz” coincidence is genuine: the Collatz–Wielandt formula is named after Lothar Collatz. But it is a general matrix/spectral theorem, historically separate from the \(3x+1\) conjecture.

### What the supplied program computes

At level \(k\), it tracks the \(3^{k-1}\) residue classes

\[
m\equiv2\pmod3\pmod{3^k}.
\]

For a positive vector \(c=(c_m)\), it implements essentially

\[
(F_\lambda(c))_m
=
\lambda^{-2}c_{4m}
+\mathbf 1_{m\equiv2(9)}
 \lambda^{\log_2(3)-2}\min_{\text{three lifts}}c
+\mathbf 1_{m\equiv8(9)}
 \lambda^{\log_2(3)-1}\min_{\text{three lifts}}c .
\]

The “three lifts” arise because information modulo \(3^{k-1}\) has three possible refinements modulo \(3^k\). Taking their minimum means:

> Make the bound valid even for the worst possible next 3-adic digit.

That is the “adversarial lost digit” description.

The program then searches for the \(\lambda\) at which the nonlinear Perron growth rate is approximately one and converts it to a counting exponent

\[
\gamma_k=\log_2\lambda_k.
\]

### An apparent sign error in the Fable

The statement

\[
\text{“KL feasibility is exactly }r(F_\lambda)\le1\text{”}
\]

appears reversed for the operator implemented by this program.

The relevant feasibility form is naturally

\[
c\le F_\lambda(c),
\]

which gives

\[
\min_i\frac{F_\lambda(c)_i}{c_i}\ge1.
\]

Moreover, the program’s computed radius decreases as \(\lambda\) increases: for \(k=2\), for example, it is above one below the threshold and below one above it. Therefore the feasible side is apparently

\[

\]

with equality at the optimal threshold. The Fable could rescue its wording by defining a reciprocal or oppositely oriented operator, but that is not what the supplied code does.

### What still needs proof

Several distinct steps are being compressed into “record broken”:

1. **Prove the exact equivalence.**
   One must explicitly eliminate the auxiliary KL variables and show that their LP constraints are precisely \(c\le F_\lambda(c)\), including the correct inequality direction.

2. **Verify the hypotheses of nonlinear Perron theory.**
   Connectivity, positivity, and possible boundary behavior need checking for every \(k\).

3. **Produce an exact certificate.**
   The current program uses NumPy `float64`. Despite comments saying “Collatz–Wielandt bounds,” it neither uses interval arithmetic nor prints a certificate. Its stopping test watches successive eigenvalue estimates, not the rigorous residual gap.

4. **Check the original KL inequalities exactly.**
   Rational or outward-rounded interval bounds must show that every one of KL’s constraints holds for a slightly smaller rational value, such as a certified
   \[
   \lambda>2^{0.8724}.
   \]

5. **Invoke Theorem 2.2 carefully.**
   Only then would one obtain the unconditional, safely rounded conclusion
   \[
   \pi_a(x)\ge x^{0.8724}
   \]
   for sufficiently large \(x\), subject to the theorem’s precise normalization and hypotheses.

### The branching-random-walk and “annealed” claims

Biggins’s theory explains how tilted Perron roots and Legendre transforms determine speeds and population exponents in genuine multitype branching random walks. That makes it a useful analogy and possibly a framework for interpreting the residue-class process. [Biggins](https://arxiv.org/abs/1003.4716)

But citing Biggins does not by itself identify the deterministic KL minimum system with a branching random walk. That correspondence needs its own theorem.

Likewise, “the annealed value is exactly \(2\)” means replacing the worst-case minimum over the three lifts by an average or expected refinement. A threshold \(\lambda=2\) would correspond to exponent

\[
\log_2 2=1.
\]

So the proposed interpretation is:

- worst-case refinement: currently about \(x^{0.872}\);
- average refinement: predicts nearly linear \(x^{1-\varepsilon}\) counting.

That is an attractive interpretation, but the supplied program contains only the minimum operator. It does not calculate or prove the annealed claim.

Finally, three increasing values do not decide whether \(\lambda_k\to2\) or stalls below \(2\). They merely make studying that limit compelling.

### Bottom line

The strongest defensible reading is:

> A compact nonlinear eigenvalue computation appears to reproduce the entire Krasikov–Lagarias table and predicts substantially better feasible exponents at \(k=12\)–\(14\). If the operator/LP equivalence is proved and the \(k=14\) vector is converted into a rigorous interval or rational certificate, it may yield an improved unconditional predecessor-count bound.

It is not yet a proof, and it certainly does not prove the Collatz conjecture. Also, “20-year record” now needs qualification: a December 2025 arXiv preprint independently claims an \(x^{0.946}\) bound, although that claim would itself require expert verification. [Counting the Collatz numbers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.13760)

Your Fable found a genuine and striking Collatz analogue, but its note mixes three layers:

1. Mahler’s proved results.
2. Straightforward consequences that are essentially correct.
3. Proposed transfers to your “Antihydra” system that still need proof.

Here is the clean version.

### 1. What is a Z-number?

A positive real number \(\alpha\) is a Z-number if

\[
0\leq \left\{\alpha\left(\frac32\right)^n\right\}<\frac12
\qquad(n=0,1,2,\ldots).
\]

Write

\[
\alpha\left(\frac32\right)^n=g_n+r_n,
\]

where \(g_n\in\mathbb Z\) and \(0\le r_n<\tfrac12\). Mahler asked whether any such \(\alpha\) exists. That remains the central unresolved issue; his paper proves necessary conditions and strong rarity results, not existence. [Mahler’s 1968 paper](https://ems.press/content/book-chapter-files/27426)

### 2. Why the ceiling map appears

Because

\[
g_{n+1}+r_{n+1}
 =\frac32(g_n+r_n),
\]

the confinement \(r_n,r_{n+1}<\tfrac12\) forces

\[
g_{n+1}
 =\frac{3g_n+\varepsilon_n}{2}
 =\left\lceil\frac{3g_n}{2}\right\rceil,
\qquad
\varepsilon_n=g_n\bmod 2.
\]

More explicitly:

- If \(g_n\) is even, confinement forces \(r_n<\tfrac13\), and \(g_{n+1}=3g_n/2\).
- If \(g_n\) is odd, confinement forces \(r_n\geq\tfrac13\), and \(g_{n+1}=(3g_n+1)/2\).

So the integer itinerary really is governed by

\[
C(g)=\left\lceil\frac{3g}{2}\right\rceil.
\]

The phrase “mirror conjugate” is exact algebraically:

\[
C(n)=-\left\lfloor\frac{3(-n)}2\right\rfloor.
\]

Thus \(n\mapsto -n\) conjugates the floor and ceiling versions.

### 3. Why consecutive odd terms are impossible

If \(g_n\) is odd, then \(r_n\geq\tfrac13\), and

\[
r_{n+1}=\frac32r_n-\frac12<\frac14.
\]

But an odd \(g_{n+1}\) would require \(r_{n+1}\geq\tfrac13\). Therefore

\[
\varepsilon_n=\varepsilon_{n+1}=1
\]

cannot occur. The parity word contains no \(11\).

Binary words of length \(t\) avoiding \(11\) are counted by Fibonacci numbers:

\[
\#\{\text{admissible words of length }t\}=F_{t+2}
\]

(up to indexing conventions). Since

\[
F_t\asymp\varphi^t,\qquad
\varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2},
\]

their proportion among all \(2^t\) binary words is approximately

\[
\left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)^t.
\]

This is the source of the golden ratio—not probabilistic drift.

### 4. What Mahler’s counting theorem actually says

Mahler converts the admissible parity/gap patterns into possible residue classes modulo \(2^t\). The number of relevant odd classes is Fibonacci-sized. Accounting for even starting integers introduces an additional factor comparable to \(t\).

Consequently, the natural sharp-form estimate coming from his argument is roughly

\[
O\!\left((\log x)x^{\log_2\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\log_2\varphi\approx0.69424.
\]

Mahler absorbs the logarithm by replacing \(0.69424\ldots\) with \(0.7\), proving that for sufficiently large \(x\), the number of Z-numbers below \(x\) is bounded by a constant-scale \(x^{0.7}\) estimate. He also proves there is at most one Z-number in each interval \([g,g+1)\), so the entire set is at most countable. [Mahler’s theorem and counting argument](https://ems.press/content/book-chapter-files/27426)

Thus the Fable’s phrase

> “hence \(\le x^{\log_2\varphi}\)”

is slightly too strong as written. The paper proves the \(0.7\) exponent; the Fibonacci calculation identifies \(\log_2\varphi\) as the underlying critical exponent, with a logarithmic factor remaining in the full count.

### 5. The “Space Needle” or valuation algorithm

Look only at the odd members \(G_k\) of the ceiling-map orbit. Starting from an odd \(G_k\), set

\[
H_k=\frac{3G_k+1}{2},
\qquad
a_k=v_2(H_k).
\]

For a possible Z-number, \(H_k\) must be even, so \(a_k\geq1\). Remove all its factors of two while compensating with powers of three:

\[
G_{k+1}
 =\left(\frac32\right)^{a_k}H_k
 =3^{a_k}\frac{H_k}{2^{a_k}}.
\]

If \(H_k\) is odd, the necessary “no consecutive odd terms” condition fails and the algorithm stops. Mahler emphasizes that indefinite continuation is necessary, not sufficient, for a Z-number.

This really is structurally Collatz-like:

- affine step \(3G+1\);
- extract an exact \(2\)-adic valuation;
- divide by the corresponding power of \(2\);
- iterate on odd states.

Calling it “the same machine” is evocative and substantially justified, provided it means structural similarity rather than equivalence of survivor sets.

### 6. The real/\(2\)-adic series—one correction

The Fable has the ratio backwards. The relevant series is

\[
S_m=\sum_{j\geq0}\varepsilon_{m+j}
       \left(\frac23\right)^j,
\]

not \(\sum\varepsilon_m(3/2)^m\). A nontrivial series with terms \((3/2)^m\) would not converge over \(\mathbb R\).

The same \(S_m\) satisfies

\[
3r_m=S_m\quad\text{in }\mathbb R,
\]

while

\[
-3g_m=S_m\quad\text{in }\mathbb Q_2.
\]

It converges in the real metric because \(|2/3|<1\), and in the \(2\)-adic metric because

\[
\left|\frac23\right|_2=\frac12<1.
\]

That is the beautiful “double completion” device: one parity sequence determines the fractional part in the real numbers and the integer part in the \(2\)-adics, with different limits.

### 7. What the proposed Antihydra transfer means

For

\[
F(n)=\left\lfloor\frac{3n}{2}\right\rfloor,
\qquad
e_j=F^j(n)\bmod2,
\]

a length-\(k\) parity word determines exactly one residue class of \(n\bmod 2^k\). This follows by reversing

\[
2n_{j+1}=3n_j-e_j,
\]

using the fact that \(3\) is invertible modulo every power of \(2\).

Therefore, if \(L_k\) is any collection of allowed length-\(k\) parity words,

\[
\#\{0\le n<2^k:\text{the itinerary of }n\text{ belongs to }L_k\}
   =|L_k|.
\]

That cylinder–residue bijection is the clean bridge. Once proved, any finite-state restriction on parity words becomes a seed-counting theorem.

If “the Antihydra counter remains in \([-1,C]\)” can be represented by a finite-state automaton with adjacency matrix \(A_C\), then

\[
|L_k|=O(\rho(A_C)^k),
\]

so, with \(k\sim\log_2 x\),

\[
\#\{\text{window-surviving seeds }\le x\}
 =O\!\left(x^{\,\theta(C)}\right),
\qquad
\theta(C)=\log_2\rho(A_C).
\]

This part is a sound research program. But the specific formula for \(\theta(C)\), and especially its claimed limiting value, depends on the exact definition and increments of the Antihydra counter; it does not follow from Mahler alone.

### Bottom line

The important discovery is real:

- Mahler already studied the ceiling-\(3n/2\) parity machine.
- Pointwise fractional-part confinement produces a finite-type symbolic constraint.
- That constraint gives Fibonacci entropy and a power-saving counting theorem.
- Parity cylinders correspond naturally to residue classes modulo \(2^k\).
- The valuation-extraction algorithm is strongly Collatz-like.

But Mahler’s Z-number condition is much stronger than ordinary ballot or cumulative-counter survival. The shared appearance of \(\varphi\) does not establish a bijection between the two survivor languages. Your Fable correctly labels that as the load-bearing gap.

So the best “first move” really is the proposed one: prove the cylinder–residue bijection cleanly, then write the exact finite-state automaton for the Antihydra window and compute its Perron eigenvalue. That would turn the analogy into a rigorous rarity theorem without claiming that Mahler already proved the Collatz-side result.

The core idea is simple, but the “day-1 lottery ticket” is actually dead on arrival.

Let \(b_i=1\) when the \(i\)-th iterate is odd and \(b_i=0\) when it is even. If Antihydra’s counter changes by

\[
+2 \quad\text{on even steps},\qquad -1 \quad\text{on odd steps},
\]

then after \(n\) steps,

\[
C_n=C_0+2n-3O_n,
\]

where \(O_n=b_0+\cdots+b_{n-1}\) is the number of odd iterates.

Assuming \(C_0=0\), reaching \(-1\) requires

\[
O_n\ge \frac{2n+1}{3}.
\]

Equivalently, relative to a half-odd baseline,

\[
O_n-\frac n2\ge \frac n6+\frac13.
\]

So Fable’s strongest valid observation is:

> Halting requires a linear upward discrepancy in the parity sequence—roughly two-thirds odd steps, rather than the “random-looking” one-half.

That is useful because ordinary random-looking fluctuations are usually only on the order of \(\sqrt n\), far smaller than \(n/6\).

### Why queueing theory appears

A Lindley recursion is the standard queueing process

\[
W_{n+1}=\max(0,W_n+x_{n+1}),
\]

which has the supremum representation

\[
W_n=\max_{0\le j\le n}\sum_{i=j+1}^{n}x_i.
\]

Thus it detects the worst excess over all suffixes or windows. Antihydra’s unreflected counter is not literally a Lindley process, but its first-passage question can be rewritten using essentially the same window-sum maxima.

The cited queueing paper derives exact worst-case values when every relevant partial sum is already constrained. Its Eq. (12) is indeed an exact optimization formula for its particular arrival/service uncertainty sets; Theorem 2 then obtains a bound uniform in the time index. It does not establish such constraints for a Collatz-type orbit. [Bandi–Bertsimas–Youssef, pp. 11–13](https://www.mit.edu/~dbertsim/papers/Robust%20Optimization/Robust%20Queueing%20Theory.pdf)

Network calculus says something similar. If every interval of length \(s\) has at most \(\alpha(s)\) arrivals, while the system supplies at least \(\beta(s)\) service, then backlog is bounded by

\[
\sup_{s\ge0}\{\alpha(s)-\beta(s)\}.
\]

These certificates can be combined across blocks or systems using min-plus convolution. [Le Boudec–Thiran, Theorem 1.4.1](https://leboudec.github.io/netcal/latex/netCalBook.pdf)

Translated back:

> If one could prove a sufficiently strong upper bound on the number of odd terms in every orbit window, the queueing machinery would turn that bound into a non-halting certificate.

But proving that window bound is the hard number-theoretic problem. Queueing theory organizes the implication; it does not supply the missing parity information.

### Why the proposed finite check cannot work

Fable proposes

\[
m_k=\max_{r\bmod 2^k}
\{\text{number of odd terms in the first \(k\) iterates from \(r\)}\}
\]

for

\[
T(n)=\left\lfloor\frac{3n}{2}\right\rfloor.
\]

But \(1\) is an odd fixed point:

\[
T(1)=\left\lfloor\frac32\right\rfloor=1.
\]

Therefore the orbit starting at residue \(1\) is odd for all \(k\) steps, so

\[
\boxed{m_k=k}
\]

for every \(k\). It can never satisfy \(m_k/k<2/3\).

Even excluding the integer \(1\) would not help: every number congruent to \(1\bmod 2^k\) has the same first \(k\) parity bits. More generally, the length-\(k\) parity vector is bijective as the starting residue ranges modulo \(2^k\). Every binary parity word occurs, including the all-odd word.

So the text almost notices its own fatal issue when it says:

> “if the parity map is bijective mod \(2^k\), the unconditioned window maxima \(m_k\) are trivial.”

It is bijective here. The “nonzero chance” of this particular test finishing the problem is therefore zero.

### The unbalance claim also needs care

In combinatorics on words, balance or unbalance concerns how evenly the 1s are distributed among equal-length factors, usually while the total number or density of 1s is fixed. Van der Laan’s thesis treats unbalance as an irregularity measure for periodic routing words and proves results about average waiting-time-type, shift-invariant multimodular functionals. [Van der Laan, Chapters 3 and 6](https://pub.math.leidenuniv.nl/~tijdemanr/proefschriftvanderlaan.pdf)

But excess density and unbalance are different:

- A word may contain two-thirds 1s and distribute them very evenly.
- An all-1 word has enormous deviation from density \(1/2\), yet no internal irregularity at its actual density.
- Average waiting time is not the same functional as first passage below zero.

Consequently,

\[
\text{halting}\Longrightarrow O_n-n/2\ge n/6+O(1)
\]

is immediate, but

\[
\text{halting}\Longrightarrow\text{standard unbalance}\ge cn
\]

does not follow without a new definition or theorem.

Likewise, “balanced density-\(1/2\) drivers never halt” needs an initial-prefix condition. A half-density word can begin with an odd step, make the counter hit \(-1\), and then become perfectly alternating forever. Bounded discrepancy only guarantees positive linear growth eventually; it does not rule out an early first passage.

### Bottom line

Fable has identified a legitimate reformulation:

\[
\boxed{\text{Antihydra halting requires a parity-prefix discrepancy of order }n.}
\]

The queueing references offer good machinery for converting a proven window-discrepancy bound into a counter bound. But:

- they do not prove the needed orbit bound;
- the proposed universal \(m_k\) computation always gives \(m_k=k\);
- standard word-unbalance results do not automatically control first passage;
- density-one statements about starting seeds say nothing decisive about the single seed \(8\).

A potentially meaningful finite-certificate search would need to restrict windows using a rigorously invariant, seed-8-specific state structure—perhaps a finite-state abstraction whose maximum cycle mean is below \(2/3\). Merely maximizing over all residues destroys precisely the arithmetic information one needs.

Several conceptual distinctions are being conflated.

### 1. Exponential versus Brunet–Derrida convergence

**Not sound without additional hypotheses.**

- Brunet–Derrida \(1/(\log N)^2\) applies to a **finite-population or cutoff approximation of a pulled, marginally selected front**. Your
  \[
  N_k=3^{k-1}
  \]
  is the number of retained residue types, not obviously the particle number or cutoff of an \(N\)-BRW. Identifying these two truncations requires a theorem, not just \(N_k\) growing exponentially.

- If that identification were valid, then indeed
  \[
  (\log N_k)^{-2}\asymp k^{-2}.
  \]

- A spectral gap plus exponentially accurate operator approximation can imply exponential eigenvalue convergence, via standard perturbation theory. But:
  - off-criticality does not by itself imply that the approximation error is exponential in \(k\);
  - criticality does not force \(k^{-2}\) for an arbitrary finite-memory/type truncation;
  - equality \(\gamma_\infty=1\) with the annealed bound is not automatically Biggins/Bramson “criticality.” Criticality normally involves a tangency/minimizer condition in the dispersion relation.

Also, a spectral gap inside the limiting operator and a gap \(2-\lambda_\infty>0\) to the annealed bound are different notions.

Finally, “adversarial minimum” is better described as worst-case control/min-plus optimization. It is not literally quenched disorder unless the digits form a fixed random environment.

### 2. What the data support

A defensible statement is:

> On \(11\le k\le19\), a geometric transient fits substantially better than an unshifted one-term \(C/k^2\) correction.

It is **not** defensible from these nine values alone to infer \(\gamma_\infty<1\), much less a ceiling near \(0.975\).

In particular, let \(e_k=1-\gamma_k\). The observed ratios are approximately
\[
\frac{e_k}{e_{k-1}}
=0.928,\ 0.933,\ 0.931,\ 0.931,\ 0.932,\ 0.934,\ 0.935,\ 0.936.
\]
Thus an exponential approach **to \(1\)**,
\[
\gamma_k=1-Cr^k,
\]
is also quite compatible with the window. A simple endpoint fit has RMS around \(7\times10^{-4}\), much better than the quoted forced unshifted \(1/k^2\) fit, though worse than the free-limit geometric fit.

Two numerical cautions:

- The last-three-point Aitken estimate is about
  \[
  0.981,
  \]
  not \(0.974\):
  \[
  \gamma_{17}-\frac{(\gamma_{18}-\gamma_{17})^2}
  {\gamma_{19}-2\gamma_{18}+\gamma_{17}}
  \approx0.981.
  \]
  Earlier local Aitken estimates vary substantially, which itself signals extrapolation instability.

- Comparing RMS values is not a statistical “rejection” unless there is an error/noise model. These are deterministic data, and the ansätze have different parameter counts.

So the evidence favors **local geometric curvature**, not a strict annealed/quenched gap.

### 3. Pre-asymptotic crossover and better diagnostics

Yes, a crossover is entirely possible. Although \(N_k\approx3.9\times10^8\), the relevant logarithmic variable is only
\[
\log N_k\approx20,
\]
and the actual memory depth is only \(k=19\). That is not necessarily asymptotic for a Brunet–Derrida law.

Moreover, the critical ansatz should generally allow a shift and subleading terms:
\[
1-\gamma_k\sim \frac{C}{(k+a)^2}
\left(1+O\!\left(\frac{\log k}{k}\right)\right).
\]
The unshifted ratio prediction \(((k-1)/k)^3\) is therefore too rigid. For example, fitting the endpoints to
\[
\gamma_k=1-\frac{C}{(k+a)^2}
\]
gives approximately \(a=13.9\) and an RMS of order \(10^{-3}\), not \(2\times10^{-2}\). That is still worse than the free geometric fit, but it removes the claim of catastrophic incompatibility.

Useful within-window diagnostics are:

1. **Forced-limit residual ratios:** examine
   \[
   q_k(L)=\frac{L-\gamma_k}{L-\gamma_{k-1}}
   \]
   for \(L=1\) and for candidate \(L<1\). Constancy indicates geometric convergence.

2. **Rolling Aitken estimates:** check whether local geometric limits stabilize as the fitting window moves. Yours do not yet stabilize strongly.

3. **Effective shifted-power parameter:** for \(d_k=\gamma_k-\gamma_{k-1}\), a shifted \(k^{-2}\) law gives approximately
   \[
   \frac{d_k}{d_{k-1}}
   \approx
   \left(1-\frac{1}{k+a-\tfrac12}\right)^3.
   \]
   Infer \(a\) from each ratio and test whether it stabilizes.

4. **Out-of-sample fits:** fit \(k=11,\dots,16\) and predict \(17,\dots,19\), rather than comparing in-sample RMS.

No finite-window diagnostic can exclude an arbitrarily late crossover without structural information.

### 4. Rigorous theorems

There is **no general theorem** saying

\[
\text{exponential convergence in }k
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\lambda_\infty<2.
\]

The implication is false abstractly: \(\lambda_k=2-c\rho^k\) converges exponentially while \(\lambda_\infty=2\).

Closest relevant results:

- **Bérard–Gouéré**, *Brunet–Derrida behavior of branching-selection particle systems on the line*, CMP 298 (2010), proves
  \[
  v_\infty-v_N\asymp(\log N)^{-2}
  \]
  for specific \(N\)-particle branching-selection systems under regularity assumptions.

- **Biggins/Bramson/Aïdékon** establish BRW speeds, boundary-case corrections, and minimum laws, but not a generic finite-type-truncation theorem for an adversarial Collatz operator.

- **Kato/Keller–Liverani spectral perturbation theory** gives a forward result: if the limiting operator has an isolated simple eigenvalue and
  \[
  \|T_k-T_\infty\|\le C\rho^k,
  \]
  then typically \(|\lambda_k-\lambda_\infty|\le C'\rho^k\). It does not imply \(\lambda_\infty<2\), and the converse is false.

For a rigorous strict gap, you would need a uniform tail bound—for example, a proved eventual contraction
\[
d_{k+1}\le q\,d_k,\qquad q<1,
\]
strong enough that
\[
\gamma_K+\frac{d_{K+1}}{1-q}<1.
\]
Absent such a bound or a model-specific truncation theorem, the current data distinguish local shapes but do not decide \(\gamma_\infty=1\) versus \(<1\).
